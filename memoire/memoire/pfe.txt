\chapter*{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

In the context of digital transformation within public administrations, human resource management has become a strategic component for improving organizational performance and enhancing administrative efficiency. The National Social Insurance Fund (CNAS) faces several challenges related to the objective evaluation of employee competencies, performance monitoring, and the centralization of HR data within the Human Resources Department (HRD).

This thesis focuses on the design and implementation of an information system dedicated to the evaluation of employee skills and performance within CNAS. The proposed solution aims to digitalize and automate the evaluation process, structure evaluation criteria, centralize employee-related data, improve traceability, and support managerial decision-making.

The adopted methodology is based on the analysis of the existing system, identification of organizational and functional requirements, UML modeling, and the implementation of a web application using modern technologies. The developed system contributes to improving transparency, consistency, efficiency, and governance in the employee evaluation process within CNAS.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords :} Information System, Human Resource Management, Performance Evaluation, Competency Management, Digital Transformation, CNAS.

\chapter*{General Introduction}
\thispagestyle{plain}

Digital transformation today influences all sectors of economic activity, including public institutions. This evolution is not limited to the simple computerization of administrative tasks; it aims above all at continuous improvement in the quality of services, reduction in processing times, data reliability, and strengthening of decision support mechanisms. In this context, human resource management occupies a strategic position, as it directly impacts work organization, institutional performance, skills development, and overall governance.

The National Social Insurance Fund (CNAS), as a large-scale national public institution, manages significant human capital, distributed across several central departments, regional agencies, and local structures. The quality of benefits provided to members depends largely on internal organizational efficiency and human resources performance. The Human Resources Department (HRD) and the Modernization and Information Systems Department (DMSI) must therefore have reliable, centralized and usable information about employees: competencies, career paths, training completed, evaluations, assigned objectives, results achieved, and development needs.

However, in many organizations, competency and performance evaluation processes still rely on heterogeneous practices: paper documents, scattered files, sometimes non-standardized criteria, and absence of a structured traceability system. These limitations reduce analysis quality, complicate individual monitoring, and slow down the implementation of workforce planning and development management.

It is in this context that our project takes place: \textbf{to design and implement an information system dedicated to the evaluation of employee skills and performance within CNAS}. The objective is to propose a digital solution allowing to structure the evaluation process, centralize HR data, and produce key performance indicators useful for strategic management, while ensuring traceability, confidentiality, and security of access.

This thesis is organized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter 1:} Presentation of the general context.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 2:} Analysis and design.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 3:} Implementation.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Presentation of the General Context}

\section{Introduction}

In this chapter, we present the overall framework of our project. We begin by introducing the host organization, CNAS, then describe the Human Resources Department (HRD) and the Modernization and Information Systems Department (DMSI). Next, we analyze the current situation regarding competency and performance evaluation, in order to identify limitations and needs. Finally, we formulate the research problem, objectives, and the proposed solution.

\section{Presentation of CNAS}

\subsection{Legal Status and General Framework}

The National Social Insurance Fund for Salaried Workers (CNAS) is a public administrative body, endowed with legal personality and financial autonomy, pursuant to Law No.\textsuperscript{o} 88-01 of January 12, 1988, relating to public bodies with administrative character. It is also presumed to be commercial in its relations with third parties, allowing it to enter into agreements and manage significant financial flows within the framework of social protection. CNAS is governed by a \textbf{Board of Directors} and placed under the supervision of the \textbf{Minister of Labor, Employment and Social Security}. Its headquarters is located in Algiers (Ben Aknoun). It has national competence and has central and local services distributed throughout the territory \cite{cnas_presentation}.

\subsection{Main Missions and Attributions}

CNAS is one of the main pillars of the social security system in Algeria. Its attributions include the management of social insurance, occupational hazards, family benefits, collection, medical supervision, and conventions with healthcare providers. This diversity of activities shows that the institution depends heavily on the quality of its internal processes and the efficiency of its human capital.

\subsection{CNAS Organization}

CNAS organization is based on a hierarchical and territorial structure that allows it to ensure national coverage. It mainly comprises:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a \textbf{General Directorate};
    \item \textbf{central services};
    \item \textbf{59 regional agencies};
    \item \textbf{768 payment structures};
    \item \textbf{health and social structures}.
\end{itemize}

This diversity has a direct consequence for our project: the evaluation system cannot be based on a uniform grid applied identically to all professions. Position families must be distinguished, criteria adapted, and comparisons limited to homogeneous areas to avoid unfair or unusable rankings. This idea aligns with research showing that a relevant evaluation system must be both standardized in its structure and adaptable according to organizational context, hierarchical levels, and job families \cite{arribas2024,kadak2023,aguinis2023}.

\subsection{Beneficiaries}

CNAS beneficiaries include salaried workers, apprentices, students, specific categories, and their dependents. This reminder highlights that the quality of benefits provided to the public is indirectly related to the quality of internal management and thus to the relevance of employee evaluation mechanisms.

\subsection{Benefits}

CNAS's main benefits cover healthcare, daily allowances, maternity insurance, disability pensions, death benefits, and occupational hazard coverage. This public service mission requires reliable, traceable, and consistent internal operation.

\section{Missions and Roles of HRD and DMSI}

\subsection{Human Resources Department (HRD)}

The \textbf{Human Resources Department (HRD)} is responsible for managing CNAS's human capital: personnel administration, recruitment, assignment, career monitoring, training, skills management, and, within the scope of this project, \textbf{performance and competency evaluation}.

Its main missions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Administrative management}: personnel file management, contracts, absences, leave, transfers, discipline;
    \item \textbf{Career management}: promotions, mobility, advancements, career path monitoring;
    \item \textbf{Training}: needs identification, training plan development, tracking and evaluation of actions;
    \item \textbf{Evaluation}: criteria definition, organization of evaluation campaigns, consolidation and exploitation of results;
    \item \textbf{HR Management}: production of dashboards, HR indicators, reports for General Management.
\end{itemize}

In this framework, HRD is not limited to administrative logic. It also participates in development objectives, clarification of professional expectations, and decision support, which aligns with research distinguishing several purposes of evaluation: administrative, developmental, role definition, and strategic \cite{lameque2023}.

\subsection{Modernization and Information Systems Department (DMSI)}

The \textbf{DMSI} plays a central role in implementing digitalization projects within CNAS. It is responsible for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the design, evolution, and maintenance of the information system;
    \item development, deployment, and support of business applications;
    \item management of hardware and software infrastructure;
    \item security of systems and data;
    \item supporting user departments in defining and implementing adapted solutions.
\end{itemize}

Within the scope of this thesis, the study and implementation of the solution takes place mainly within DMSI, in close functional coordination with HRD.

\section{Theoretical Framework and Concepts Related to the Field of Study}

\subsection{Information System (IS) and HRMS}

An \textbf{information system} groups together all resources (software, data, procedures, hardware, and actors) allowing to collect, store, process, and distribute information. A \textbf{Human Resource Management System (HRMS)} is an information system specialized in HR management. It centralizes personnel information and automates processes such as administrative management, training, mobility, career monitoring, and evaluation.

In a modern logic of human resource management, HRMS does not only serve to archive data. It becomes a support for analysis, monitoring, coordination between actors, and decision support. In our case, it constitutes the technical foundation allowing to structure evaluation, secure its data, and improve its exploitation.

\subsection{Competency}

A \textbf{competency} designates a structured set of knowledge, skills, and behaviors that can be mobilized in a work situation. Within our project, we distinguish technical competencies, behavioral competencies, and, depending on positions, managerial competencies.

However, competency should not be viewed as an abstract or purely generic notion. Recent research on \textit{competency models} shows that a credible system must be based on explicit, structured referentials adapted to the organization's context. In other words, competencies must be selected, organized, and linked to position families to make evaluation coherent, usable, and comparable within homogeneous areas \cite{arribas2024}. This approach justifies, in our project, the establishment of differentiated referentials according to professions and evaluation profiles.

\subsection{Performance}

\textbf{Performance} reflects the level of objective achievement and the quality of work completed. Campbell and Wiernik show that it is multidimensional: it includes task performance, execution quality, discipline, cooperation, and, for certain positions, leadership dimensions or contextual contribution \cite{campbell2015}. This vision moves us away from simplistic rating and justifies the use of structured grids, weighted criteria, and multi-indicator dashboards.

Within CNAS's context, this conception is particularly important. Professions are varied, constraints differ from one department to another, and performance cannot be reduced to simple quantitative output. It must be appreciated through results, but also through professional behaviors, procedural compliance, cooperation, and execution quality.

\subsection{Competency and Performance Evaluation}

Evaluation is a structured process aimed at:
\begin{itemize}
    \item measuring individual performance over a given period;
    \item assessing competency levels and their evolution;
    \item identifying training and support needs;
    \item proposing development plans.
\end{itemize}

DeNisi and Murphy recall that evaluation should not be reduced to an annual rating, but thought of as a performance management system articulating objectives, feedback, measurement, development, and management \cite{lameque2023,aguinis2023}. In this perspective, the logic of \textit{performance management} goes beyond the act of evaluating to integrate a broader cycle comprising objective setting, regular monitoring, feedback production, results analysis, and employee development support.

This approach is particularly suited to an organization like CNAS, where evaluation cannot only serve to rank or punish, but must also support activity regulation, needs identification, and overall quality of HR management.

\subsection{Feedback, Perceived Justice, and Biases}

Research on feedback shows that its effects are not automatically positive. Some forms of feedback can improve performance, while others can degrade it if poorly designed. Moreover, recent work on continuous feedback shows that feedback mediated by a person, particularly by the hierarchical manager, produces more favorable effects on performance, motivation, and engagement than purely technological or impersonal feedback \cite{giamos2024}. Literature also emphasizes that quantitative feedback and qualitative feedback do not have exactly the same effects: scores and indicators allow measurement and comparison, while comments and observations allow explanation, guidance, and support for improvement \cite{giamos2024}.

In an evaluation system, it is therefore relevant to combine:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{quantitative feedback}, based on ratings, scores, and indicators;
    \item \textbf{qualitative feedback}, based on comments, observations, and assessments.
\end{itemize}

Furthermore, evaluation is exposed to several biases: leniency, severity, central tendency, halo effect, recency, or implicit comparison. In a multi-actor system, the perception of justice and process clarity thus play a central role in device acceptability. Research on \textit{performance appraisal justice} indeed shows that a system perceived as just strengthens employee commitment, engagement, and indirectly their performance \cite{lyu2023}. This idea justifies the integration, in our project, of traceability mechanisms, hierarchical validation, dispute resolution, and audit.

\subsection{KPIs and Dashboards}

\textbf{KPIs} (key performance indicators) allow managing HR activity: campaign completion rate, processing delays, volume processed, rework rate, competency scores, note dispersion, note inflation by manager, or gaps between observed and target competency. Provided documents show that an indicator is only relevant if it is defined, traceable, compared within a coherent area, and actionable.

In our project, KPIs are not viewed as mere display figures. They constitute management instruments allowing HRD, managers, and administration to identify gaps, track campaign progress, analyze results by position families, and support decision-making.

\section{Compliance, Data Protection, and Profiling}

HR evaluation processes personal data and may constitute profiling when used to analyze or predict work performance aspects. Law 18-07 and its 2025 amendment impose a compliance-by-design logic: designation of a DPO, maintenance of a treatment register, automated operations log, technical and organizational security measures, and strict access control \cite{loi1807,loi2511}. This has a direct translation into system architecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item role-based and scope-based access control;
    \item logging of consultations, modifications, and exports;
    \item data and transfer security;
    \item maintenance of usable traceability;
    \item minimum score explainability.
\end{itemize}

Beyond regulatory obligation, this compliance logic also strengthens system credibility, improves user trust, and supports procedural justice perception.

    \item the \textbf{DMSI}, which ensures technical study, design, and solution implementation.

The proposed information system mainly covers:

    \item definition and management of referentials;

    \item centralization and historization of evaluation results;

\subsection{Analytical Scope}

Provided documents insist on the need to distinguish position families. Priority indicators are not the same for a file/counter-service position, an IT/DMSI position, an HR function, a managerial position, or a health/social structure. The system must therefore remain parameterizable, versioned, and capable of producing intra-family comparisons rather than cross-profession comparisons.

This adaptation principle aligns with research showing that an effective evaluation system must be sufficiently standardized to remain coherent, but sufficiently adaptable to account for organizational specificities, hierarchical levels, and different work contexts \cite{aguinis2023,kadak2023}.

\section{Study of the Current Situation}

\subsection{General Findings}

Before implementing a dedicated system, evaluations may rely on scattered supports (paper documents, Excel sheets, email exchanges) and non-standardized criteria. This situation creates several difficulties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{lack of uniformity} of criteria and evaluation grids;
    \item \textbf{absence of unique referential} of competencies;
    \item \textbf{limited traceability} of evaluations;
    \item \textbf{manual consolidation} of results, source of errors and wasted time;
    \item \textbf{difficulty in producing reliable statistics}.
\end{itemize}

These findings show that traditional evaluation practices, when based on scattered documents and manual processing, allow neither continuous performance monitoring, nor strategic data exploitation, nor satisfactory integration of feedback. They often produce results that are poorly comparable, difficult to audit, and weakly usable from HR decision perspective.

\subsection{Current Process (Example of Workflow)}

Generally, an evaluation process follows these steps:
\begin{itemize}
    \item preparation of evaluation grids and criteria;
    \item communication to hierarchical managers and concerned services;
    \item conducting interviews and manual input of evaluations;
    \item transmission of results to HRD;
    \item consolidation and archiving;
    \item exploitation of results.
\end{itemize}

\section{Research Problem}

Given the study of the current situation, the general research problem can be formulated as follows:

\begin{quote}
\textit{How to design and implement an information system allowing to standardize, automate, and secure the process of evaluating employee competencies and performance within CNAS, in order to improve analysis quality, decision traceability, and strategic management of human resources?}
\end{quote}

The major difficulties to resolve are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the absence of a shared competency and criteria referential;
    \item the dispersion and centralization of evaluation data;
    \item the burden of manual consolidation;
    \item the difficulty in producing synthetic indicators;
    \item the need to integrate compliance and security constraints from the design phase.
\end{itemize}

This research problem is part of a broader dynamic of transforming evaluation practices towards integrated performance management systems, capable of responding to simultaneously operational, organizational, and strategic purposes.

\section{Proposed Solution}

To address this research problem, we propose the implementation of an \textbf{internal web application} dedicated to competency and performance evaluation within CNAS. The proposed solution notably includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a centralized evaluation database;
    \item a structured referential of competencies and evaluation criteria;
    \item configurable grids according to position families;
    \item an input and validation workflow;
    \item dashboards and reports for HRD, managers, and administration;
    \item user and role management ensuring confidentiality;
    \item an audit log, treatment register, and complete operation traceability.
\end{itemize}

The proposed solution does not merely consist of digitizing an existing evaluation form. It aims to establish a structured logic of performance management, in which evaluation is connected to referentials, position profiles, indicators, a feedback process, and decision-making exploitation. In this sense, it responds to several purposes of evaluation: administrative purpose, development purpose, professional expectation clarification purpose, and strategic purpose \cite{lameque2023}.

\section{General Description of the Solution}

As a reminder, our solution consists of creating a web platform dedicated to evaluating employee competencies and performance within CNAS.

This platform is not limited to recording scores. It has been designed as a continuous performance management system, structured around several complementary dimensions: referential configuration, campaign management, evaluation input, self-evaluation, score calculation, validation tracking, indicator production, audit, compliance, and reporting. This orientation aligns with recent approaches where evaluation should be integrated into a broader \textit{performance management} process, articulating objectives, measurement, feedback, tracking, and decision-making exploitation.

This platform mainly includes the following interfaces:
\begin{itemize}
\item Authentication interface;
\item User and role management interface;
\item Organization management interface (agencies, structures, services, employees);
\item Evaluation campaign management interface;
\item Profiles and evaluation criteria management interface;
\item Evaluation input interface;
\item Self-evaluation interface;
\item Results consultation interface;
\item Dashboards and reports interface;
\item Audit and compliance interface.
\end{itemize}

These interfaces aim to:
\begin{itemize}
\item automate the evaluation process;
\item centralize data related to employees, campaigns, and evaluations;
\item define evaluation criteria and competency referentials;
\item assign evaluations to hierarchical managers;
\item allow input, validation, tracking, and historization of evaluations;
\item integrate self-evaluation into the overall process;
\item produce scores, indicators, reports, and dashboards;
\item improve traceability, transparency, and exploitation of results;
\item guarantee security, access control, and auditability of sensitive operations.
\end{itemize}

This solution is designed as a modular platform, where referentials, organization, campaigns, evaluations, scores, dashboards, and compliance functions are clearly separated. This separation facilitates maintenance, access control, audit, system scalability, and alignment with different evaluation purposes: administrative, developmental, strategic, and professional expectation clarification.

\section{Requirements Specification}

The purpose of this phase is to capture system context, identify technical and functional requirements, identify stakeholders involved in our system and their functions, and define the logical structure of the solution.

\subsection{Organizational Needs}

\begin{itemize}
\item Respect CNAS hierarchical organization.
\item Improve task distribution among HRD, hierarchical managers, and employees.
\item Ensure centralized and traceable management of evaluation campaigns.
\item Facilitate tracking, consolidation, and exploitation of results.
\item Provide differentiated functioning according to position families.
\item Allow meaningful comparisons within homogeneous areas.
\item Promote clarity of the evaluation process to improve acceptability.
\item Integrate multiple evaluation purposes: HR management, decision support, development, and tracking.
\end{itemize}

These organizational needs are particularly important in CNAS's context, where profession diversity, hierarchical levels, and structure variety impose a system that is both consistent in structure and adaptable in application. Indeed, the same evaluation grid cannot be applied indiscriminately to a counter agent, an IT professional, an HR function, or a managerial position without producing weak or unfair comparisons.

\subsection{Technical Requirements}

These are determinative requirements for the system. Our system must guarantee:
\begin{itemize}
\item ease of use;
\item secure authentication;
\item access control according to roles and hierarchical areas;
\item a centralized database ensuring information preservation and exploitation;
\item an audit log tracing sensitive operations;
\item a modular architecture facilitating system evolution;
\item integration of a treatment register;
\item form versioning logic;
\item controlled and auditable exports;
\item exploitable traceability of consultations, validations, corrections, and disputes.
\end{itemize}

The solution must also be capable of handling calculation mechanisms, historization, reporting, and data filtering according to roles. In this sense, it must be designed as an evaluation system, but also as a management and governance system for HR data.

\subsection{Functional Requirements Capture}

\subsubsection{Identification of Actors}

An actor represents any entity external to the application, whether a human user or another system, that directly interacts with it.

For our system, we identified the following actors:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Administrator}: manages users, roles, certain structure parameters, and supervises general platform administration;
\item \textbf{HRD}: conducts campaigns, manages referentials, tracks results, exploits indicators, processes certain validations, and participates in compliance;
\item \textbf{Hierarchical Manager}: evaluates employees in their area, consults self-evaluations, validates or rejects evaluations at hierarchical level, and tracks team activity;
\item \textbf{Employee}: consults results, completes self-evaluation, tracks history, and can initiate certain requests, particularly in case of correction or dispute.
\end{itemize}

This actor distribution reflects multi-actor logic. It allows better responsibility distribution, improves information quality, and strengthens device justice perception. It aligns with inclusive logic, where multiple perspectives intervene in the evaluation cycle.

\subsubsection{Functional Requirements}

System functionalities are defined as the needs, actions, and reactions that the system must undertake in response to a primary actor's request.

The system must allow the following actors to:
\begin{itemize}
\item authenticate to the system;
\item manage users;
\item manage roles and access rights;
\item manage agencies, structures, services, and employees;
\item create an evaluation campaign;
\item open, close, and track a campaign;
\item define evaluation criteria;
\item define evaluation profiles;
\item define competency referential;
\item assign evaluations to hierarchical managers;
\item input evaluation scores and comments;
\item save an evaluation as draft;
\item submit an evaluation;
\item validate or reject an evaluation according to responsibility level;
\item complete a self-evaluation;
\item consult evaluation history;
\item consult individual and consolidated results;
\item generate reports;
\item export results;
\item consult dashboards;
\item record important actions in an audit log;
\item manage notifications;
\item manage disputes, corrections, or requests related to compliance when authorized;
\item ensure traceability of validations, rejections, exports, and sensitive operations.
\end{itemize}

At business level, these functionalities must support not only evaluation recording, but also overall process tracking, results exploitation, feedback quality, and trust in the device.

\subsection{Scoring System and Indicators}

Provided resources show that a credible evaluation system must not stop at a raw score. It must at least distinguish performance score, competency score, and overall score, while explaining the main factors contributing to the result.

In our system, scoring logic relies on multiple levels:
\begin{itemize}
\item a \textbf{score by criterion}, obtained from the entered score;
\item a \textbf{score by axis}, obtained by weighted aggregation of multiple criteria;
\item a \textbf{performance score}, mainly based on quantitative, procedural, or assiduity dimensions;
\item a \textbf{competency score}, based on qualitative, behavioral, self-evaluative, or managerial dimensions;
\item an \textbf{overall score}, obtained by combining the two previous levels according to job family and evaluation profile.
\end{itemize}

This scoring architecture presents several advantages:
\begin{itemize}
\item it avoids reducing the process to a single score;
\item it makes evaluation more explainable;
\item it allows adapting weightings to position families;
\item it distinguishes observed results from mobilized capacities and behaviors;
\item it facilitates building analytical dashboards.
\end{itemize}

Indicators can include, depending on position families, volume processed, deadline compliance, cycle time, rework rate, file completeness, perceived feedback quality, procedural compliance, technical competency score, behavioral competency score, and leadership score.

However, these indicators must not be used without caution. They must be operationally defined, linked to a traceable data source, compared within a coherent area, and integrated into role-adapted dashboards. In particular, comparisons should be privileged within homogeneous populations rather than between very different professions.

\subsection{Questionnaires and Measurement Quality}

Support documents emphasize that a serious evaluation system must distinguish performance and competency, provide parameterizable forms, and rely on minimal questionnaire validation.

In practice, this means:
\begin{itemize}
\item review of items by subject matter experts;
\item pretest on a small sample;
\item acceptable internal consistency;
\item documentation of the questionnaire used in each campaign;
\item clear distinction between quantitative, qualitative, behavioral, and managerial criteria;
\item explanation of weightings and calculation rules.
\end{itemize}

This logic improves device credibility and strengthens the thesis's scientific quality. It also allows avoiding two frequent errors: confusing competency and performance, and producing a final score without documenting its components.

Moreover, measurement quality is directly related to system acceptability. A well-constructed questionnaire, understandable, consistent with the evaluated position, and clearly linked to expected criteria reduces ambiguities, facilitates feedback, and strengthens the device's justice perception.

In this chapter, we presented the analysis and design of our competency and performance evaluation system within CNAS. We clarified organizational, technical, and functional needs, identified actors, formalized main use cases, then structured design around a modular architecture coherent with project requirements.

We also showed that the system is not based solely on technical logic, but on an articulated set of business choices: adaptation to position families, multi-actor workflow, explainable scoring, questionnaire quality, audit, compliance, and management by indicators. The mobilized scientific literature, as well as provided resources, allowed strengthening this approach by distinguishing performance, competency, feedback, perceived justice, and compliance. \section{Working Environment}

In this section, we detail hardware and software tools as well as technologies used for development.

\subsection{Technologies Used}

\paragraph{React:}
React is a modern component-oriented JavaScript library used to build dynamic and responsive user interfaces. In our project, React was chosen to structure the interface in reusable components, improve frontend maintainability, and facilitate screen management dedicated to different system roles \cite{react2025}.

\paragraph{TypeScript:}
TypeScript is a typed superset of JavaScript that adds static typing, advanced autocompletion, and better safety during development. Its use in our application helps reduce errors, clarify data contracts exchanged with the API, and improve overall frontend code quality \cite{typescript2025}.

\paragraph{Python:}
Python is a versatile programming language, widely used for web development, automation, and data processing. Within the context of this application, Python forms the basis of backend development through its readability, productivity, and rich ecosystem \cite{python2025}.

\subsection{Frameworks and Database}

\paragraph{Django:}
Django is a high-level web framework based on Python. It provides a robust architecture for developing secure, maintainable, and rapidly deployable applications. In our project, Django was used to implement business logic, organize data models, manage authentication, and expose necessary services to the React interface \cite{django2025}.

\paragraph{PostgreSQL:}
PostgreSQL is an open source relational database management system, recognized for its robustness, SQL compliance, and reliability. It was chosen to ensure centralized storage of HR data, campaigns, evaluations, results, and traceability logs. Its relational model is particularly suited to the integrity, reporting, and historization needs of our system \cite{postgresql2025}.

\paragraph{Chosen Technical Architecture:}
The realized solution relies on a modern client-server architecture: the user interface is developed with React and TypeScript, the backend is implemented with Python and Django, and data is stored in PostgreSQL. This technology combination meets the modularity, maintainability, security, and scalability needs highlighted in the analysis reports provided for the thesis.

\section{Application Architecture}

The application is organized into several layers:
\begin{itemize}

This modular architecture allows separating navigation logic, business logic, and data persistence.

\begin{itemize}

\item filtering by hierarchical area;
\item traceability of access and exports;
\item maintenance of exploitable history;

\end{itemize}

\chapter*{General Conclusion}
\thispagestyle{plain}

This thesis addressed the design and implementation of an information system dedicated to evaluating employee competencies and performance within CNAS. Through this work, we first analyzed the organizational context and identified limitations of existing practices: criteria heterogeneity, scattered supports, weak traceability, consolidation difficulty, and weak decision value of results.

We then showed that designing such a system cannot be reduced to simple numerical transposition of a paper form. It requires work framing position families, referentials, indicators, validation workflows, measurement quality, and data governance. The scientific literature mobilized, as well as provided resources, allowed strengthening this approach by distinguishing performance, competency, feedback, perceived justice, and compliance.

Finally, UML modeling, the relational model, modular architecture, and implementation choices allowed proposing a coherent, scalable system foundation adapted to CNAS needs. In perspective, this work could be extended by even more advanced dashboards, manager calibration mechanisms, better questionnaire formalization, dispute modules, and an even more complete compliance layer.
